The Calvinese language (siehelehinhitsi or CALVINESE) a alphabetical code language written and spoken almost exlcusively in the Kouz Islands. Calvinese is based on the Latin alphabet and emerged largely as a resistance to British rule which began in the late 19th century. As the independence movement grew in the wake of World War I, the language emerged as a way for islanders to communicate one another without speaking English. The language benefitted from it's simplicity and root in the latin alphabet which allowed for a common language that bridged generations without directly speaking English. Additionally, it centralized many prior dialects that were also spoken in the region. The popularity of the language boomed, with many Khouzan religious leaders translating religious texts into Calvinese. Today, Khouzan religious leader maintain that religious texts must be taught in Calvinese.

Upon Kouz independence, fluency has declined throughout the island chain. This decline was exacerbated by the secularist movement in the early 2000s, where activists associated the language with religious instruction. In turn, English is now widely spoken in Calvinvill. However, there has been a recent incentive by the Calvinvill government to bolster Calvinese language learning. In Khouza, largely due to the continuity of religious instruction, Calvinese is still pominent.


Alphabet

The Calvinese alphabet is based on the Latin alphabet and consists of 26 letters. Each letter in the Calvinese alphabet is based on the phoentic pronunciation of the Latin alphabet. This means each 'letter' in the Calvinese alphabet often consists of a 'chunk' of two English letters. Below is the standardized Calvinese alphabet, as there have been different past iterations.

Grammar

Since the Calvinese language acts like an alphabetical code, English grammar remains largely the same when translating. However there are some key differences. When a single letter repeats within a word a single apostrophe replaces the following letter.

For example:

Test --> ti'tsti; instead of tiitsti


When a 'chunk' repeats (like the Calvinese letter E) the repeating chunk is replaced with a "

For example:

Book --> beou"kh; instead of beououkh


Yet, with words that end in the same letter (like see or tree), what apostrophe to use can be ambiguous. However, the " should be used since the chunk is repeating.

For example:

See --> tsi"; instead of tsii


Calvinese punctuation mirrors English punctuation. However, since a singular apostrophe and quotation marks are used as grammar indicators in Calvinese, French guillemets are used when quoting someone or something. Since each letter in the Calvinese alphabet consists of two English letters, abbreviations are translated into Calvinese using the first letter of the corresponding Calvinese letter.


Translator

Calvinese is typically written in all lowercase, so despite the input, the output will be lowecase. The translator will immediately convert the quotes to guillemets. All other English punctuation will be preserved. Most importantly, do not use contractions, translations will be incorrect.
